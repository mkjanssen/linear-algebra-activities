Section 3.2 Oct 15: The Complete Solution to \(Ax=b\)

Subsection 3.2.1 Warmup

Warmup 3.2.1.
Determine if the following collections of vectors are subspaces of an appropriate \(\mathbb{R}^m\text{.}\) Let \(A\) be a fixed \(3\times 3\) matrix.
  1. \(\displaystyle W = \setof{\begin{pmatrix} a+2c \\ 2a + b +3c \\ a + b+c\end{pmatrix}}{a,b,c \text{ are in } \R}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle U = \setof{\begin{pmatrix} a\\ b \\ c\end{pmatrix}}{a+3b-c=2}\)
  3. \(\displaystyle V = \setof{\begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \\ 1\end{pmatrix}}{a,b \in \R}\)
  4. \(\displaystyle Y = \setof{\mathbf{v} \text{ in } \R^3}{A\mathbf{v} = 0}\)

SageMath has a built-in method that looks like “Matrix division”. Here we “left divide” by the matrix. This is odd notation, and is just something SageMath allows.

It is weird, but this works even if A is not invertible, like now.

Subsubsection 3.2.2.3 Method Three: Another SageMath Built-in

Finally, SageMath will also try to solve the system if you apply the .solve_right() method to A. You have to supply the vector b as an argument to the command.

Again, this only pulls out a single particular solution. It is up to you to figure out the rest.

Subsection 3.2.3 Explorations

Exploration 3.2.1. [Strang Ex. 3.3.4].
Find the complete solution (also called the general solution) to
\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 & 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 6 & 4 & 8 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \\ t \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Exploration 3.2.2. [Strang Ex. 3.3.6].
What conditions on \(b_1\text{,}\) \(b_2\text{,}\) \(b_3\) and \(b_4\) make each of these systems solvable? Find a solution in those cases.
  1. \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \\ 2 & 5 \\ 3 & 9 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ b_3 \\ b_4 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
  2. \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3\\ 2 & 4 & 6\\ 2 & 5 & 7\\ 3 & 9 & 12\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ b_3 \\ b_4 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Exploration 3.2.3. [Strang Ex. 3.3.11].
It is impossible for a \(1 \times 3\) system of equations to have \(x_p = (2,4,0)\) and \(x_N = \text{ any multiple of } (1,1,1)\text{.}\) Explain why.
Exploration 3.2.4. [Strang Ex. 3.4.24].
Give examples of matrices \(A\) for which the number of solutions to \(Ax = b\) is
  1. \(0\) or \(1\text{,}\) depending on \(b\text{;}\)
  2. \(\infty\text{,}\) regardless of \(b\text{;}\)
  3. \(0\) or \(\infty\text{,}\) depending on \(b\text{;}\)
  4. \(1\text{,}\) regardless of \(b\text{.}\)
