Subsection 7.1.1 Sage and Diagonalization

Sage has built-in commands about diagonalization. We shall try a few out here. We need a matrix to play with, so we take this one:

We chose to define this matrix over AA because we need to find roots of polynomials when looking for eigenvalues. AA is the set of algebraic numbers, which just means the collection of all roots of polynomials with integer coefficients.

Sage has a command for finding the eigenvector decomposition \(A = S\Lambda S^{-1}\text{.}\)

As you see, Sage returns a pair of matrices. One of them is diagonal, so that is probably \(\Lambda\text{.}\) We'll use tuple unpacking to assign the matrices to sensible names.

Note that \(S\) has the eigenvectors of \(A\) as its columns, and the corresponding eigenvalues are lined up as the diagonal entries of \(\Lambda\text{.}\)

Subsection 7.1.2 Explorations

Exploration 7.1.1.
Are the following matrices diagonalizable? Justify.
\begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 1 \\ 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix}, \ B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -4 & -6 \\ -1 & 0 & -3 \\ 1 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix}, \ C = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 4 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 & 2\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
Exploration 7.1.2.

A discrete (linear) dynamical system \(x_{k+1} = A x_k\text{,}\) which describes the long-term behavior of a state vector \(x_k\text{,}\) is best understood via the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(A\text{,}\) as described in this example.

In the redwood forests of California, dusky-footed wood rats provide up to 80% of the diet for the spotted owl, the main predator of the wood rat. Let's build a linear model to describe the basic physical system of the owls and the rats.

Denote the owl and wood rat populations at time \(k\) by the vector \(x_k = \begin{bmatrix} O_k \\ R_k \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) where \(k\) is the time in months, \(O_k\) the number of wols in the region studied, and \(R_k\) the numer of rats (measured in thousands). Suppose

\begin{align*} O_{k+1} &= (0.5) O_k + (0.4)R_k\\ R_{k+1} &= (-p) O_k + (1.1)R_k, \end{align*}

where \(p\) is a positive parameter to be specified.

  1. Ignoring the \(R_k\) term in the first equation, what does \((0.5) O_k\) mean? Answer similar questions for the other three terms on the right-hand sides of the equations.
  2. Assume \(p=0.104\text{.}\) Write a coefficient matrix \(A\) satisfying \(x_{k+1}= A x_k\text{.}\)
  3. Find the eigenvalues and associated eigenvectors of \(A\text{.}\)
  4. Diagonalize \(A\text{,}\) if possible.
  5. Let \(v_1, v_2\) be the eigenvectors of \(A\text{.}\) Why can any initial state \(x_0\) be written as a linear combination \(x_0 = c_1 v_1 +c_2 v_2\text{?}\)
  6. Now explain why \(x_k = c_1 A^k v_1 + c_2 A^k v_2\) for any \(k > 0\text{.}\) Use whatever numbers you've found in place of the letters.
  7. As \(k\to \infty\text{,}\) what does \(x_{k+1}\) approach (in terms of \(x_k\))?
Exploration 7.1.3.
Suppose \(A\) is \(5\times 5\) with two eigenvalues. One eigenspace is three-dimensional while the other is two-dimensional. Is \(A\) diagonalizable?
Exploration 7.1.4.
Suppose \(A\) is \(3\times 3\) with two eigenvalues. Each eigenspace is one-dimensional. Is \(A\) diagonalizable?
Exploration 7.1.5.
Suppose \(A\) is \(4\times 4\) with three eigenvalues. One of the eigenspaces is one-dimensional, and one of the others is two-dimensional. Is it possible that \(A\) is not diagonalizable?
Exploration 7.1.6.

Let \(e_1, e_2, e_3\) be the standard basis of \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} e_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad e_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad e_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}

Make an example of an invertible \(3 \times 3\) matrix \(S\text{.}\) Write your matrix as a matrix of column vectors.

\begin{equation*} S = \begin{pmatrix} | & | & | \\ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \\ | & | & | \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

How do you know that the set \(\{ Se_1, Se_2, Se_3 \}\) is a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{?}\)

What is the connection between \(Se_1\text{,}\) \(Se_2\text{,}\) \(Se_3\text{,}\) \(S^{-1}v_1\text{,}\) \(S^{-1}v_2\text{,}\) \(S^{-1}v_3\) and the original vectors \(e_1, e_2, e_3, v_1, v_2, v_3\text{?}\)

Finally, how do we use this to understand the way that the decomposition \(A = S\Lambda S^{-1}\) works?
