Exploration 8.2.2.
As it turns out, we can also use matrices to describe linear transformations between non-euclidean spaces using coordinates. Let's see how using differentiation \(D: \mathbb{P}_2 \to \mathbb{P}_2\) of quadratic polynomials as an example.
  1. Consider the general quadratic \(ax^2 + bx + c\text{.}\) Why can the subspace \(\mathbb{P}_2\) of quadratics be represented by the basis \(\mathcal{B}=\{x^2, x, 1\}\text{?}\)
  2. What is the image under \(D\) of each of the basis vectors?
  3. Under the basis \(\mathcal{B}\text{,}\) what is the coordinate vector that describes a general quadratic?
  4. What are the coordinates of the basis vectors? How about their images under \(D\text{?}\)
  5. Verify that when the matrix for \(D\) is applied to the coordinate vector for \(x^2 - x -1\) that the coordinate vector for \(2x-1\) is obtained.
