Exploration 7.1.6.

Let \(e_1, e_2, e_3\) be the standard basis of \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} e_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad e_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad e_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}

Make an example of an invertible \(3 \times 3\) matrix \(S\text{.}\) Write your matrix as a matrix of column vectors.

\begin{equation*} S = \begin{pmatrix} | & | & | \\ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \\ | & | & | \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}

How do you know that the set \(\{ Se_1, Se_2, Se_3 \}\) is a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{?}\)

What is the connection between \(Se_1\text{,}\) \(Se_2\text{,}\) \(Se_3\text{,}\) \(S^{-1}v_1\text{,}\) \(S^{-1}v_2\text{,}\) \(S^{-1}v_3\) and the original vectors \(e_1, e_2, e_3, v_1, v_2, v_3\text{?}\)

Finally, how do we use this to understand the way that the decomposition \(A = S\Lambda S^{-1}\) works?
