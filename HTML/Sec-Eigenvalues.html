Subsection 6.2.2 Explorations

Exploration 6.2.1.
Compute the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(A\) and \(A^{-1}\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 2 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
How are the eigenvalues of \(A\) and \(A^{-1}\)related?
Exploration 6.2.2.
Find (by hand) the eigenvalues of \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(A+B\text{,}\) where
\begin{equation*} A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, B = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Is it true that eigenvalues of \(A+B\) are equal to eigenvalues of \(A\) plus eigenvalues of \(B\text{?}\)
Exploration 6.2.3.
Find the eigenvalues of \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) \(AB\text{,}\) and \(BA\text{,}\) where
\begin{equation*} A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, B = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
  1. Are the eigenvalues of \(AB\) equal to the eigenvalues of \(A\) times eigenvalues of \(B\text{?}\)
  2. Are the eigenvalues of \(AB\) equal to the eigenvalues of \(BA\text{?}\)
Exploration 6.2.4.
A Markov matrix has columns that sum to 1 and are useful in probabilistic applications. It is a theorem that an \(n\times n\) Markov matrix \(M\) with strictly positive entries has a steady state vector \(q\) in the sense that \(\lim\limits_{k\to\infty} M^k x_0 = q\) given any initial state \(x_0\text{.}\) Consider the Markov matrix:
\begin{equation*} M = \begin{pmatrix} 0.6 & 0.2 \\ 0.4 & 0.8 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
  1. Choose any initial state \(x_0\text{.}\) Calculate the first ten terms of the sequence \(M^k x_0\text{.}\) What appears to be happening?
  2. Let's verify the behavior you observed. Find the eigenvalues of \(M\text{.}\) What are their associated eigenvectors?
  3. What is the steady state limit \(q\text{?}\)
